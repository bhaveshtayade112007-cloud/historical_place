Taj Mahal - The Eternal Monument of love

About the Taj Mahal

What is it? The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum located on the right bank of the river Yamuna in the Indian city of Agra. It was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favourite wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. The Taj Mahal is widely recognized as "the jewel of Muslim art in India" and remains one of the world's most celebrated structures and a symbol of India's rich history.

Location & History: The Taj Mahal is situated in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India, approximately 200 kilometers south of New Delhi. Construction began in 1632 and was completed in 1653, employing over 20,000 artisans and craftsmen from across the empire and Central Asia. The complex was designed by a board of architects led by the court architect to the emperor, Ustad Ahmad Lahauri. The project took approximately 22 years to complete and cost an estimated 32 million rupees at the time.

Historical Importance: The Taj Mahal is considered the finest example of Mughal architecture, a combination of Islamic, Persian, and Indian styles. It was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 for being "the jewel of Muslim art in India and one of the universally admired masterpieces of the world's heritage." The monument attracts 7-8 million visitors annually and has become a symbol of India's cultural heritage and architectural excellence. It represents the pinnacle of Mughal architectural achievement and stands as a testament to the empire's wealth and artistic sophistication.

Interesting Facts:

  • The Taj Mahal changes color throughout the day - it appears pinkish in the morning, milky white in the evening, and golden at night when illuminated by the moon.
  • The four minarets surrounding the main tomb are tilted slightly outward to protect the main dome from damage in case of an earthquake.
  • The calligraphy verses from the Quran inscribed on the walls create an optical illusion - the letters appear to be the same size from top to bottom, despite being gradually larger as they go higher.
  • The entire complex is perfectly symmetrical, except for the two cenotaphs inside the tomb - Shah Jahan's is slightly larger than Mumtaz Mahal's.
  • It's said that Shah Jahan planned to build a second Taj Mahal in black marble across the river as his own tomb, but his plans were interrupted by a war with his sons.
